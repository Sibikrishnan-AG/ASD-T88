What is Behaviour Therapy?

Behavior therapy is a non-pharmacological intervention used to treat ADHD by improving a child's behavior, emotional regulation, and focus, without the reliance on medication.

Why practice Behavioural Therapy?

A national health authority from the UK conducted a clinical trial to evaluate the effectiveness of various ADHD treatments. Studies have shown that behavioral therapy, particularly through parent and/or classroom training programs, is the most effective approach for children younger than 7. This method is less invasive and helps children who are still developing to avoid potential long-term effects of medication.

What are the steps to take?

The health authority published guidelines and best practices parents can adopt:

• Use incentives or rewards to encourage your child for good behavior and performance. This can motivate the child to practice positive actions consistently.

• Enforce disciplinary actions by firmly educating the child on the negative consequence for problematic behaviour.

• Use a simple and calm tone when communicating with your child. Children with ADHD are often sensitive to their environment, so ensure your instructions are clear, easy to understand, and authoritative to help them follow through.

What makes communication effective?

Depending on the individual's age severity and ADHD subtype diagnosed, speech and comprehension deficiency may vary. Typically, children with ADHD have consistent basic cognitive (i.e. thinking and understanding) and intellectual (i.e. reading and language) abilities, with what is expected for their chronological age, what they tend to struggle handle complicated action task related to comprehension, organization, and focus.

For that reason, children with ADHD often struggle with emotional regulation and pragmatic social communication dysfunction.

E.g. Interrupting conversations, blurting out responses before the speaker finishes their conversation.

Families can use the following pointers to effectively communicate with their ADHD children:
• Use simple and specific terms to convey instructional message. You can consider breaking down complex instructions into smaller parts.
• Ensure surrounding has minimal to no distractions.
• Train children to maintain eye contact with the speaker, this is one strategy to get child to focus and listen to the speaker.
• Use visual cues to demonstrate steps, as children are more likely to engage with visual supports, which make complex concepts easier to understand and help improve information retention through illustrations or animations

Why does environment matter to people with ADHD?

ADHD is a neurodevelopmental disorder that affects brain circuits involved in regulating aspects of daily life, such as dopamine levels, emotions, focus, and hyperactivity. Since it is a genetic condition, children who inherit the disorder will show signs of impairment, and these symptoms may persist into adulthood.

Studies have shown that external influences from an individual's environment can impact the severity of ADHD. Factors such as parenting approaches, social contexts, and learning environments can influence the presentation and management of ADHD symptoms.

Impact on younger children

Poor environmental factors can be detrimental to young children who are still developing, directly or indirectly affecting their quality of life and long-term well-being.

Examples of such impact include difficulties in maintaining relationships with family and peers, unstable emotions, and poor performance at school.

In extreme cases, as they reach adulthood, these individuals may develop personality disorders, engage in criminal behavior, and struggle to cope in the workplace.

How to create a conducive environment?

Parenting approach

• Be patient with your child to allow them to learn and grow at their own pace. This helps to reduce stress, build trust, and reinforce positive behaviors.
• Find a balance in your parenting style, being overly-authoritarian or overly-permissive may not contribute positively to child's growth.
• Practice effective communication with child to allow them to express their feelings openly, providing emotional support and listening ear for them. Environmental exposure

• Reduce exposure to harmful substances (e.g. pesticides, chemical substances containing lead, air pollution). Such chemicals can alter brain development by contributing to inflammation, oxidative stress, and disrupting normal neurological processes.
• Minimise distractions and noise or visual disturbances to create a stress-free and focused space.
• Encouragechild to have an active lifestyle promotes physical health but also helps improve focus, impulse control, and emotional regulation.
